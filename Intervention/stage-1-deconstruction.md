
# Stage 1: Deconstructing Algorithmic Authority (The De-naturalization Phase)
‌
Objective: To raise critical awareness about "Algorithmic Flattening" and the loss of epistemic agency.
‌
### Key Activities:

The "Spot the Erasure" Exercise: Students are shown two versions of a text: one written by an L2 author with rich cultural metaphors, and one "cleaned" by AI. They must identify what was lost (the "silenced" voice).
Critical Lectures: Introduction to the concept of AI as a 'statistical parrot' rather than a 'truth-teller'.
Discussion: How does AI-mediated writing reinforce Western academic norms while erasing local knowledge systems?
‌
Outcome: Students move from Epistemic Submission to Critical Skepticism.
‌

# Stage 2: Reclaiming the Authorial Voice (The Agency Training Phase)
‌
Objective: To equip students with the linguistic and psychological tools to "talk back" to the algorithm.
‌
### Key Activities:
Metadiscourse Negotiation: Students learn to use "Resisting Prompts" (see Appendix E) to set boundaries for the AI.
Voice Reconstruction: Rewriting AI-generated output to re-insert personal identity markers and cultural nuances.
The "Why" Protocol: Students must justify every AI suggestion they accept. If they cannot explain why a change is better, they must reject it.

Outcome: Students develop Negotiated Epistemic Agency.

# Stage 3: Recursive Reframing (The Consolidation Phase)
‌
Objective: To transform the DAR model into a long-term writing habit.
‌
### Key Activities:
Collaborative Peer-Review: Students review each other's AI-mediated drafts, specifically looking for "flattened" language or overly-sanitized arguments.
Reflective Journaling: Writing about the shift in their relationship with AI: "Who is the master of this text?"
Final DAR Portfolio: Submission of a final paper alongside a 'Negotiation Log' showing the history of their interactions with the AI.
‌
Outcome: Students achieve Strategic Epistemic Autonomy.
